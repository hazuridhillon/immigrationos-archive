Overview

A Sole-Source Contract for the Infrastructure of Mass Deportation

ImmigrationOS is not a new idea. It is the culmination of a 14-year, $287 million relationship between ICE and Palantir Technologies — built across four administrations, and now accelerated into a real-time deportation targeting machine.

In April 2025, ICE awarded Palantir Technologies a $30 million sole-source contract to build the Immigration Lifecycle Operating System (ImmigrationOS) — an AI-powered platform designed to identify, target, track, and deport individuals at scale. ICE cited an "urgent and compelling" national security need, and declared Palantir the only possible vendor.

The system is built on Palantir's existing Investigative Case Management (ICM) platform, which has served as the official system of record for all ICE investigative cases since 2014. That proprietary foundation is precisely why no other vendor could bid: ICE had already committed its entire enforcement architecture to Palantir's codebase.

ImmigrationOS ingests data from across the federal government — IRS tax records, Social Security files, passport databases, DMV records, Medicaid enrollment data, license plate readers — and assembles them into real-time profiles of deportation targets. Its field-facing tool, ELITE (Enhanced Leads Identification & Targeting for Enforcement), allows agents to draw geographic shapes on a map and select "target-rich" neighborhoods for raids.

The platform was prototyped by September 25, 2025, with the contract running through September 2027. A concurrent $29.9 million modification renewed operations and maintenance. By late 2025, Palantir had also begun a separate contract with USCIS — placing it on both the enforcement and benefits sides of the immigration system simultaneously.

The story is not simply about a government contract. It is about how a private company's proprietary software became irreplaceable state infrastructure — and what that means for democratic accountability, civil liberties, and the people caught in the system.

Timeline of the ICE–Palantir Relationship

2011–2026
2011 — Obama Administration

Initial Contract: Homeland Security Investigations

Palantir begins work with ICE's Homeland Security Investigations (HSI) division — centered on investigating transnational criminal organizations. This framing as criminal investigation, rather than deportation support, will remain central to Palantir's public positioning for over a decade.

2014 — Obama Administration

$41M ICM Contract: The Foundation Is Built

ICE awards Palantir a $41 million contract to build and maintain the Investigative Case Management (ICM) system — modernizing ICE's legacy TECS database. ICM gives every ICE agent access to a unified network of federal and private databases, combining immigration history, employment records, biometrics, family relationships, license plate data, and social media profiles. ICE describes ICM as "mission critical." $41,000,000 — Initial ICM contract

2014 — Concurrent

FALCON: Search, Analysis, and Field Operations

Palantir also builds FALCON — a suite of data analysis and visualization tools for ICE agents, including FALCON-SA (Search and Analysis), FALCON-DARTTS (trade-based crime), and FALCON-Roadrunner (weapons trafficking). FALCON enables agents to connect subject records across jurisdictions, conduct telephone searches, and link cases across departments.

2017 — Trump Administration I

ICM Reaches "Final Operation Capacity" — Used Across Enforcement Divisions

Documents obtained by The Intercept disprove Palantir's claim that its tools are used only for criminal investigations. In 2017, HSI and ERO conduct a joint operation using ICM to arrest family members of undocumented children caught at the border. ICM reaches "final operation capacity" under the first Trump administration.

2019 — Trump Administration I

Mississippi Raids; Internal Protest; Palantir Declines ERO Contracts

HSI deploys FALCON to coordinate raids on food-processing plants in Mississippi — nearly 700 people arrested. Concurrently, Palantir employees protest the company's ICE work. In 2020, Palantir writes to Amnesty International stating it has "purposefully declined" ERO and CBP contracts due to human rights concerns — drawing an explicit line it will later erase.

2022 — Biden Administration

$90M Renewal: The Lock-In Deepens

ICE renews the Palantir contract for five years at approximately $90 million. The contract ID — 70CTD022FR0000170 — is the same task order that will later be modified to create ImmigrationOS. FALCON is retired. By this point, the proprietary architecture that will later justify sole-source procurement is firmly entrenched. $90,000,000 — 5-year renewal

April 2025 — Trump Administration II

ImmigrationOS: The $30M Sole-Source Contract

ICE modifies task order 70CTD022FR0000170, citing J&A-25-0092. The justification: urgent and compelling need; Palantir is the only possible source. A March 2025 executive order simultaneously compels agencies to share data via Palantir's Foundry platform, institutionalizing Palantir as the connective tissue of federal data sharing. $30,000,000 — ImmigrationOS contract

September–October 2025

Prototype Delivered; Renewed; ELITE Deployed; USCIS Contract Signed

ImmigrationOS prototype delivered on deadline. ICE immediately issues J&A-25-0309 ($29.9M) for O&M, switching justification from "urgent need" to "only one source." ELITE is deployed. A USCIS "VOWS" platform contract is signed, placing Palantir on both the enforcement and benefits sides of immigration. $29,900,000 — O&M renewal

December 2025

Washington Post: Karp's Ideological Shift Revealed

The Washington Post reports that CEO Alex Karp — formerly a Democrat who said mass deportation "makes no sense" — has declared Palantir "completely anti-woke" and championed Trump's crackdown. Palantir's September 2025 federal contract total reaches $128 million — its largest monthly sum on record.

January 2026

ELITE User Guide Leaked; Medicaid Data Confirmed; Congress Demands Answers

404 Media publishes ELITE's internal user guide, confirming Medicaid records, DMV files, and utility data feed address confidence scores. Agents draw shapes on maps to designate "target-rich areas" for raids. Congressional representatives demand transparency from Palantir. Total ICE–Palantir payments reach $287 million.

The Three Operational Pillars

As stated in ICE contract justification documents
01

Targeting & Enforcement Prioritization

Streamlines identification and apprehension based on ICE enforcement priorities — affiliates of transnational criminal organizations, visa overstays, and "violent criminals." The ELITE app operationalizes this pillar in the field, converting data profiles into geospatial raid plans. Defined verbatim in J&A-25-0092.

02

Self-Deportation Tracking

Provides "near real-time visibility" into instances of voluntary departure from the United States. Critics note this creates a surveillance mechanism for people who have already left — enabling monitoring of the voluntary departure pipeline and potential re-targeting upon return. Per J&A-25-0092.

03

Immigration Lifecycle Operations

Manages the complete end-to-end process from identification through removal — including deportation logistics and transportation resource allocation. Internal Palantir wiki documents describe work supporting deportation logistics. The system treats deportation as an optimization problem with throughput as the metric.

System Architecture & Data Sources

Technical capabilities and data integration
Data Integration

Cross-Agency Data Fusion

ImmigrationOS and its predecessor ICM integrate data from across the federal government: IRS tax records, Social Security files, passport databases, DMV records, Medicaid enrollment, utility bills, and license plate reader data. A March 2025 executive order formalized this by mandating agencies share data via Palantir's Foundry platform. Palantir also participated in DOGE efforts to build a "mega API" at the IRS, and in September 2025 the Treasury Department awarded Palantir a contract to create a "unified API layer."

Sources: ICE J&A-25-0092 · 404 Media · American Immigration Council · The Hill
Field Targeting

ELITE: Geospatial Raid Planning

ELITE populates a map with deportation targets, assigns each person a "confidence score" for their current address based on Medicaid and government data, and allows agents to draw geographic shapes to identify "target-rich" neighborhoods. It represents a shift from individual tracking to neighborhood-scale enforcement — raising fundamental questions about probable cause and Fourth Amendment protections.

Sources: 404 Media ELITE user guide · Biometric Update · Fortune
Case Management

ICM: The Backbone System

The Investigative Case Management system is the official system of record for all HSI investigative cases. It enables agents to make connections between subject records across multiple databases, conduct telephone and address searches, link cases across jurisdictions, and interface with CBP systems for lookout and seizure tracking. Every ICE agent has access to it. ICM has been described by ICE as "mission critical" and is built entirely on Palantir's proprietary platform, making it impossible to transfer to another vendor without rebuilding from scratch.

Sources: EPIC litigation · ICE J&A-25-0092 · ACLU
Logistics

Deportation Lifecycle Management

The Immigration Lifecycle Operations pillar manages the physical logistics of deportation at scale — overlaying information about detained or removed individuals against the availability of transportation resources. Internal Palantir wiki documents describe work on deportation logistics including transport allocation. ICE detention reached a record 73,000 people as these systems were deployed.

Sources: 404 Media Slack leaks · NYU Stern Center · AFSC Investigate

Civil Liberties & Legal Concerns

Documented risks and ongoing debates

Fourth Amendment & Probable Cause

ELITE's probabilistic "confidence scores" drive neighborhood-scale raids rather than address-specific certainty. The Fourth Amendment requires probable cause for searches and seizures. Critics argue an AI-generated probability score does not constitute probable cause — yet it is directing enforcement operations. More than a dozen US citizens have been erroneously detained or deported during the 2025 crackdown.

Healthcare Data as Surveillance

Using Medicaid records to identify deportation targets creates a profound chilling effect on healthcare-seeking behavior. KFF analysts note it may not be technically possible to isolate undocumented individuals in Medicaid data — meaning the dragnet sweeps up legal residents and potentially citizens. The EFF described it as ICE "stalking people for arrest" using healthcare data.

Vendor Lock-In & Democratic Accountability

Because ImmigrationOS is built on Palantir's proprietary code, no other vendor can bid for or maintain this work. The design choices embedded in ImmigrationOS — which data to ingest, what triggers an alert, how confidence scores are weighted — are enforcement policy decisions made without public deliberation, congressional authorization, or independent oversight.

Algorithmic Bias

No independent audit of ImmigrationOS's accuracy or bias has been made public. Precedent from COMPAS — a criminal justice AI found nearly twice as likely to incorrectly flag Black defendants as high-risk — demonstrates how algorithmic systems replicate and amplify societal biases. The consequences here are deportation, family separation, and detention.

Conflict of Interest

Stephen Miller, the Trump administration's chief architect of immigration policy, holds a substantial financial stake in Palantir — underscoring conflicts of interest in the government's embrace of the company's technology. The Project on Government Oversight raised this as a significant ethics concern, noting Miller's policy decisions directly increase demand for Palantir's product.

Human Rights Due Diligence

Amnesty International's August 2025 correspondence challenged Palantir to demonstrate adequate human rights due diligence before entering and renewing ERO contracts — particularly given the company's own UN Guiding Principles-based human rights policy. Amnesty argued that given Palantir's prior acknowledgment of these risks (2020 letter), the company "could and should have known" about foreseeable harms.
